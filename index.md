## Working Papers

### Interactions between Direct Air Capture and the electricity market

with Knut Einar Rosendahl
{:.paper-authors}

R&R at Resource and Energy Economics
{:.paper-status}

Direct Air Capture (DAC) might become an important technology in the future, due to a need of negative CO2 emissions. DAC operation is very energy intensive, and large-scale use of DAC will thus impact the electricity market. Meanwhile, electricity generation is becoming more volatile with bigger share of intermittent technologies. This paper examines possible interactions between the electricity market and adoption of DAC technologies. Combining theoretical analysis with stylized numerical simulations, we show that DAC adoption can increase or decrease electricity price volatility, depending on the choice of DAC technology. Cost reductions or improved energy efficiency for the most energy-efficient technology tends to increase price volatility, as it reduces the deployment of the more energy-intensive DAC technology, which is more likely to be turned off when prices are high. Further, higher CO2 prices increase the deployment of the most energy-efficient technology to a larger degree than the more energy-intensive one.

### Econometric Modelling of Norwegian CO2 emissions

This paper develops an econometric model of Norwegian domestic CO2 emissions over 1990–2024, using annual data on disaggregated fossil fuel consumption, real GDP and capital stock. Adopting the climate econometrics framework of Hendry (2020) and Castle and Hendry (2022), the analysis considers ARDL and Error Correction specifications and applies small-sample cointegration tests. Since these tests do not support cointegration over the main sample, the focus shifts to a first-difference model of short-run emissions dynamics. Oil consumption emerges as the principal driver of annual changes in CO2 emissions, with coal also contributing positively, while evidence on natural gas and real GDP is weaker. Multi-step projections to 2030 indicate a continued emissions decline, though at a slower pace than the sharp reductions observed after 2019. A robustness check using a longer 1977–2023 sample finds evidence of cointegration once measurement breaks are accounted for, highlighting sensitivity to sample length and data definitions.

### The role of residual emissions for the abatement choice of hard-to-abate industries

with Knut Einar Rosendahl and Franziska Holz
{:.paper-authors}

Reaching net-zero emissions is challenging in sectors where it is difficult to completely eliminate emissions due to long investment cycles, high energy use, and low-profit margins. This paper explores cost-minimizing abatement options and includes the costs for residual emissions, i.e., for the CO2 emissions that may remain after firms implement abatement technologies. Firm-level emission sources are considered when selecting abatement technologies in the hard-to-abate industrial sectors steel, cement, and chemicals, which are notable for their significant contributions to CO2 emissions. Two main decarbonization pathways are identified, i) carbon capture and storage (CCS), and ii) the use of low-carbon fuels or technology/process modifications. Emitting firms must consider the CO2 price for residual CO2 emissions when making abatement choices, as these emissions carry long-term cost implications that affect the overall effectiveness and feasibility of net-zero strategies. We provide an analytical model to depict the arbitrage between CCS and non-CCS abatement, and examine potential welfare losses if the CO2 price in the market is suboptimal. Our numerical application incorporates data for Germany, the largest emitter within the EU.
